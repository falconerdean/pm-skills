NAUTICAL SLANG - COMMON EXPRESSIONS 
"Cut and Run" - was the practice of securing sails of a square-rigged ship with old, 
disposable rope so it could be cur free for a quick departure. Boaters today most oli:en 
think the phrase refers to cutting the anchor line for a quick escape. 
''Dressing Down" - today this phrase refers to a reprimand for an officer or sailor. In 
earlier times, thin or worn sails were treated with oil and wax, which was called dress­
ing down. 
"Hard-up" - when the ships wheel was turned hard over to one side, the wheel could 
seize-up. With no way to relieve the seizing, the sailor was said to be hard-up or met 
with misfortune. 
'Junk" - we use this word today for things thar have no personal value. l n seafaring 
days, junk referred co old rope not able co take a load. The rope was usually cut into 
pieces to make mops and mats. 
"Overhaul" - was the need to send crew alofi: to haul ropes/lines over rhe sails, called 
overhauling, to prevent the sails from being chaffed. 
"Scuttlebutt'' - was a water barrel with a hole cur into it so sailors could scoop out 
drinking water. The ship's gossip was ofi:en shared around the scuttlebutt. 
"Taken Aback" - when the wind is on the wrong side of the sails and forces the ship 
astern, the ship is said co be taken aback, perhaps due co an inarrentive helmsman. 
"Tide Over" - in the absence of wind, a sailing ship would Aoar with the tide until 
the wind returned, called a tide over. Today the phrase refers ro a small supply used 
until one's main supply arrives. 
"Under the \%ather" - during seafaring days, this meant keeping watch on the 
windward or weather side of the bow. Sailors assigned ro rhis station often became 
soaked with sea water and fell ill.

The City of Bainbridge was previously named Winslow, raking 
its name from Winslow Hall, a shipbuilder and founding brother 
of Hall Brothers Shipyard. Prior to 1903, the area was called 
Madrone, but after the Hall brothers moved their operation to

Winslow, which had been incorporated in 1947, annexed the rest

ofBainbridge. 
The city now occupies the entire space of Bainbridge Island
